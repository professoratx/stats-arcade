Orientation

Specificity answers the mirror-image question to sensitivity: of all the patients who truly do NOT have the disease, what fraction does the test correctly clear?

A high-specificity test rarely cries wolf — it almost never produces a false positive on a healthy patient. So when a high-Sp test comes back positive, you can trust that positive. That's why specificity is the rule-in engine.

The article reports Sp = 0.98 — well above the 70% threshold and into excellent territory. Let's see what that means for the patients who get a positive antigen result.

Right now the grid shows the article's value: Sp = 0.98, so 98 cleared and 2 false alarms (per 100 disease-free).

SpP In

A useful memory aid for what specificity is for: when Sp is high and the test comes back Positive, you can rule the disease In. In other words, a high-specificity test rarely produces false positives — so a positive result really does mean disease.

This means that, if you suspect a specific condition exists in your patient, you would use a highly specific test to rule in the condition. For example, the article's Sp = 0.98 is excellent. Only 2 of every 100 disease-free patients get a false positive — so when the antigen test says positive, it's almost certainly right.

Sp = TN / (TN + FP)
Sp = 392 / (392 + 8) = 392 / 400 = 0.98|

★ So what does Sp = 0.98 mean?

Of every 100 patients who truly do NOT have COVID, this test correctly clears 98 and falsely flags 2. If you're using a positive result to rule COVID in, you'll be wrong only about 2 times in every 100 disease-free patients. That's the upside of a 98% specific test — a positive result is highly trustworthy. The 2 false alarms still cost something (extra confirmation, brief worry), but the ratio is excellent for most clinical scenarios.

4
Four rules for reading specificity in any paper

Specificity is reported on essentially every diagnostic accuracy paper. These four rules carry through whatever test you're reading about:

• Sp = TN / (TN + FP). Just the disease− column. The denominator is everyone who actually does NOT have the disease.
• SpPIn. High Sp + Positive result = disease ruled In. The mnemonic clinicians use to remember what Sp is for.
• The 70% threshold. Below 70%, a positive result isn't trustworthy enough to rule in disease. Above 70%, it starts being useful. Above 90%, very trustworthy. Like sensitivity, specificity is a sliding scale — here it tells you how much you can trust a positive.
• Specificity doesn't tell you what to do with a negative. A high-Sp test rules in when positive. To know whether to trust a negative result, use sensitivity (Part 4) and NPV (Part 6).

The most important habitWhen you read "Sp = X.XX," translate it immediately to "out of every 100 people without the disease, this test correctly clears X and falsely alarms on the rest." That's the patient-level meaning.

Big takeaways

What specificity will tell you in any paper

  • Sp = TN / (TN + FP). The fraction of truly disease-free patients the test correctly clears. Disease− column only.
  • SpPIn. High Sp + Positive result rules disease In. That's what specificity is for.
  • Translate Sp directly to people. Sp = 0.98 means “98 of every 100 disease-free patients get a correctly negative result; 2 get a false alarm.” That's the patient-level read.
  • Sp is independent of prevalence and Sn. Same disease− column gives the same specificity at any prevalence, and changing TP or FN won't move Sp one bit.
  • Above 70% trustworthy, above 90% very trustworthy. The article's 0.98 is excellent — a positive antigen result is very likely a true positive.
