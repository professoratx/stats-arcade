From the gold standard to the foundation underneath every accuracy number

You've just seen what a gold standard is and why we compare quick tests to it. Now let's look at where the six diagnostic accuracy values come from — a single small table that captures every patient's outcome on a test, set against what was actually true.

The six values you'll learn to read

Every diagnostic accuracy paper reports some combination of these six statistics. You'll meet each one in detail in Parts 2–6. They all share one structure underneath, which we'll get to in a moment.

Accuracy
Overall Accuracy
The fraction of all test results that are correct. Sounds great, but can mislead — especially in populations where disease is rare or common.
▸ Part 2
Prevalence
How common the condition is
The proportion of patients in a population who have the condition. Doesn't measure the test itself — but bends what its results mean.
▸ Part 3
Sn
Sensitivity
Of patients who truly have the condition, how many does the test catch? High Sn = trust a negative result (rule-out).
▸ Part 4
Sp
Specificity
Of patients who truly don't have the condition, how many does the test correctly clear? High Sp = trust a positive result (rule-in).
▸ Part 5
PPV
Positive Predictive Value
Of patients who tested positive, how many actually have the condition? Answers the patient's question after a positive result.
▸ Part 6
NPV
Negative Predictive Value
Of patients who tested negative, how many are actually clear? Answers the patient's question after a negative result.
▸ Part 6

The 2 × 2 table — the foundation

All six diagnostic accuracy values come from one shared structure: a small table that compares what the test said to what's actually true — with truth established by the gold standard you just read about. Every patient ends up in exactly one of four cells:

The columns are what's actually true — does the patient have the condition or not? The rows are what the test result said — positive or negative. Where the row and column intersect tells you the kind of outcome that patient experienced.

Same four cells — four real patients

Four kinds of outcomes, one busy clinic

Imagine a single afternoon at a community urgent-care clinic. Four patients come in with respiratory symptoms during flu season. Each gets a rapid antigen test for COVID. The results, set against what was actually true (confirmed later by PCR), look like this:

TP
Marcus, 28
Came in with fever, body aches, and a cough that started two days ago. Antigen test was positive. PCR sent the next day confirmed COVID. The test caught what was actually there.
→ Sent home to isolate, stayed away from his asthmatic mother. Outcome: appropriate.
FP
Eileen, 64
Came in with a stuffy nose, mild sore throat, no fever. Antigen test was positive. PCR the next day was negative; a respiratory panel later confirmed she actually had a different (not-COVID) virus. The test rang an alarm that wasn't there.
→ Cancelled her granddaughter's birthday trip; isolated for three days unnecessarily. Real cost, even though she was never sick with COVID.
FN
David, 41
Came in feeling tired with a slight headache. Antigen test was negative, so he went back to work. Three days later he was much sicker; PCR came back positive. The test missed what was actually there.
→ Two coworkers caught COVID from him before he knew he was contagious. The negative result felt like a green light, but it wasn't.
TN
Priya, 35
Came in with seasonal allergies — itchy eyes, runny nose, no fever. Antigen test was negative. She didn't have COVID; she really did just have allergies. The test correctly cleared her.
→ Skipped the unnecessary isolation, made it to her daughter's recital. Outcome: appropriate.
The cells that keep clinicians up at night

Which errors matter, and when?

True positives (Marcus) and true negatives (Priya) are the cells you want — the test got it right. The two error cells (Eileen and David) are the ones that drive how clinicians actually use diagnostic tests. Different errors matter for different clinical decisions.

Ruling out — confirming a patient is clear
When you can't afford to miss the disease
When the consequence of missing a true case is severe (David walking around contagious, an early dementia case slipping through, an unsafe driver cleared as fine), you want to be sure that a negative result really means negative. The error you fear is the false negative.
Watch this cell: FN — and Sensitivity (Part 4) is the statistic that tells you how big this cell is.
Ruling in — confirming the patient has it
When you can't afford to falsely alarm
When acting on a positive result is invasive, expensive, or distressing (Eileen cancelling her trip, sending a patient for a full neuropsychological battery, a difficult diagnostic conversation), you want to be sure that a positive result really means positive. The error you fear is the false positive.
Watch this cell: FP — and Specificity (Part 5) is the statistic that tells you how big this cell is.
