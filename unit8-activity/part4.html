Orientation

Sensitivity answers one question: of all the patients who truly have the disease, what fraction does the test catch? It's the test's batting average against actual cases.

A high-sensitivity test rarely misses true cases — so when it comes back negative, you can trust that negative. That's why sensitivity is the rule-out engine.

The article reports Sn = 0.70, which sits right at the threshold where a test starts being clinically useful for ruling out. Let's see what that means for the patients on the receiving end.

Right now the grid shows the article's value: Sn = 0.70, so 70 caught and 30 missed (per 100 truly infected).

SnN Out

A useful memory aid for what sensitivity is for: when Sn is high and the test comes back Negative, you can rule the disease Out. In other words, a high-sensitivity test rarely produces false negatives — so a negative result really does mean negative.

This means that, if you're trying to confidently rule a condition out in your patient, you would use a highly sensitive test. For example, the article's Sn = 0.70 is only borderline. 30 of every 100 truly infected patients get a falsely reassuring negative — so a negative antigen result rules COVID out only modestly, and symptomatic patients often need PCR follow-up.

Compute it from the table, then click the card

The formula uses just the two cells from the disease+ column: TP and FN. That's it.

Sn = TP / (TP + FN)
Sn = 70 / (70 + 30) = 70 / 100 = 0.70|

Now click the dashed Sensitivity card on the left side to confirm the value and see the 95% CI.

★ So what does Sn = 0.70 mean?

Of every 100 patients who truly have COVID, this test catches 70 and misses 30. If you're using a negative result to rule COVID out, you'll be wrong about 3 times in every 10 truly infected patients. That's the trade-off baked into a 70% sensitivity. Whether that's acceptable depends on what the missed cases cost — for COVID in symptomatic patients, missing 30% is a significant clinical concern, which is why most clinicians treat antigen tests as a screening tool rather than a definitive rule-out.

4
Four rules for reading sensitivity in any paper

Sensitivity is reported on essentially every diagnostic accuracy paper. These four rules carry through whatever test you're reading about:

• Sn = TP / (TP + FN). Just the disease+ column. The denominator is everyone who actually has the disease.
• SnNOut. High Sn + Negative result = disease ruled Out. The mnemonic clinicians use to remember what Sn is for.
• The 70% threshold. Below 70%, a negative result isn't trustworthy enough to rule out disease. Above 70%, it starts being useful. Above 90%, very useful. Sensitivity isn't a yes/no; it's a sliding scale of how much you can trust a negative.
• Sensitivity doesn't tell you what to do with a positive. A high-Sn test rules out when negative. To know whether to trust a positive result, you need specificity (Part 5) and PPV (Part 6).

The most important habitWhen you read "Sn = X.XX," translate it immediately to "out of every 100 people with the disease, this test catches X." That's the patient-level meaning.

Big takeaways

What sensitivity will tell you in any paper

  • Sn = TP / (TP + FN). The fraction of truly diseased patients the test correctly identifies. Disease+ column only.
  • SnNOut. High Sn + Negative result rules disease Out. That's what sensitivity is for.
  • Translate Sn directly to people. Sn = 0.70 means “out of every 100 people with the disease, this test catches 70 and misses 30.” That's the patient-level read.
  • Sn is independent of prevalence and Sp. Same disease+ column gives the same sensitivity whether prevalence is 1% or 50%, and changing FP or TN won't move Sn one bit.
  • The 70% threshold is a useful floor, not a verdict. Above 70%, a negative starts being trustworthy; above 90%, very trustworthy; below 70%, a negative result can't safely rule out disease.
