Step 2 · Mock journal article
Diagnostic accuracy of an over-the-counter rapid antigen test for SARS-CoV-2 in symptomatic adults at a community urgent-care clinic
Abstract — Background

Rapid antigen tests for SARS-CoV-2 are widely available over the counter and provide results within 15 minutes, but their performance compared with reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) testing varies across populations and clinical settings. We evaluated the diagnostic accuracy of a commercially available rapid antigen test in symptomatic adults presenting to a community urgent-care clinic during a period of active community transmission.

Methods

We conducted a prospective diagnostic accuracy study of 500 symptomatic adults (aged 18–75) presenting consecutively to a community urgent-care clinic with respiratory symptoms (cough, fever, sore throat, or congestion) of less than seven days' duration. Each patient received a single-use rapid antigen test (collected via anterior nasal swab) followed immediately by a reference RT-PCR test from a paired nasopharyngeal swab. Tests were performed by trained clinical staff; antigen results were read at 15 minutes per manufacturer instructions, and PCR results were processed at a regional reference laboratory within 24 hours. The reference RT-PCR result served as the gold standard. Test sequence and reading were blinded to the comparison result.

Results

Of 500 symptomatic patients tested, 100 (20.0%) were positive on RT-PCR, indicating a study prevalence of prevalence = 0.20, 95% CI [0.17, 0.24]. The contingency of antigen and PCR results is shown below.

The rapid antigen test correctly classified 462 of 500 patients (overall accuracy = 0.92, 95% CI [0.90, 0.94]). Of the 100 patients with PCR-confirmed SARS-CoV-2, the antigen test correctly identified 70 (Sn = 0.70, 95% CI [0.60, 0.79]). Of the 400 patients without SARS-CoV-2 on PCR, the antigen test correctly classified 392 as negative (Sp = 0.98, 95% CI [0.96, 0.99]). Among the 78 patients with a positive antigen result, 70 were truly infected (PPV = 0.90, 95% CI [0.81, 0.95]). Among the 422 patients with a negative antigen result, 392 were truly uninfected (NPV = 0.93, 95% CI [0.90, 0.95]).

Conclusions

In this prospective study of symptomatic adults at a community urgent-care clinic during active community transmission, the rapid antigen test demonstrated high specificity but moderate sensitivity relative to RT-PCR. A positive antigen result has a high probability of being correct, supporting use as a rule-in test in symptomatic populations. A negative antigen result, however, does not reliably exclude SARS-CoV-2 infection: approximately 30% of truly infected patients were missed by the antigen test. Clinicians should consider follow-up RT-PCR testing in symptomatic patients whose antigen test is negative, particularly when clinical suspicion remains high or when correct diagnosis is essential for public health or treatment decisions.
