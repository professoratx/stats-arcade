Welcome to Unit 8

Reading diagnostic accuracy like a clinician

Every test you order, recommend, or read about in OT and audiology comes with a set of numbers that tell you how good it is at finding disease — and how much you can trust the result you're holding. Sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, prevalence: these aren't just terminology to memorize. They're how you decide whether to act on a test result, follow up with another test, or reassure a patient.

This activity walks you through six diagnostic accuracy concepts — one at a time, anchored to a single mock article — then asks you to apply all of them to a different clinical scenario. By the end, you'll be able to read any diagnostic accuracy paper with confidence and translate the findings to a patient or colleague.

Why diagnostic accuracy matters for OT and AuD

You will spend your career making decisions based on imperfect tests. A pure-tone audiogram, a hand grip dynamometer, a cognitive screen, a vestibular bedside test, a balance assessment — every one of them sometimes says “positive” when the patient is healthy, and sometimes says “negative” when the patient has the condition. That's not a flaw of medicine. It's the nature of testing.

What separates a clinician who reads tests well from one who doesn't is not whether they know the numbers exist, but whether they know which numbers to trust under which circumstances. Should you act on a positive screen, or follow up with a more definitive test? When a patient tests negative but you still suspect the condition, what does that mean? When the prevalence in your clinic is much higher than in the general population, how does that change your reading?

Six numbers — the ones in this activity — answer those questions.

One more thing before we start: what's a “gold standard”?

You'll see this term constantly in diagnostic accuracy work, so it's worth pinning down now. The gold standard is the most authoritative test available for a condition — the test we treat as the source of truth when we're judging how good a different test is. Every diagnostic accuracy statistic in this activity is built on a comparison: what the test under study said, versus what the gold standard said.

Why don't we just use the gold standard for everyone, then? Because gold standards usually cost something other tests don't — time, money, equipment, expertise, patient burden, or all of the above. The whole point of having a less authoritative test is that it's faster, cheaper, more accessible, or less invasive. The accuracy numbers tell you how much you're giving up to get those benefits.

In our running scenario

In Parts 2–6, we'll work with a study comparing an over-the-counter rapid antigen test for COVID against RT-PCR (the gold standard for COVID diagnosis). PCR is more accurate, but it has to be sent to a lab, takes hours to a day to come back, and isn't something a patient can do at home. The antigen test gives a result in 15 minutes on a kitchen counter — if we can trust it. That's the question every diagnostic accuracy statistic is built to answer.

A quick interactive moment

The same logic shows up in OT and AuD all the time. Tap each button below to see a quick “screening test vs. gold standard” pair from your field. You'll need to view both to continue.

In Occupational Therapy — cognitive screening
The quick test
Brief cognitive screen (e.g., Mini-Cog or SLUMS)
Takes 5–15 minutes during a home-safety eval. An OT can administer it. Scores flag patients who may need follow-up.
The gold standard
Comprehensive neuropsychological battery
Multi-hour evaluation by a neuropsychologist. Considered the source of truth for cognitive impairment, but expensive, scheduled out weeks, and not portable.
Why the quick test exists: a 5-minute screen during a routine home visit can flag the patients who need the gold-standard workup — without sending every older adult for a full neuropsych battery they don't need.
In Audiology — hearing screening
The quick test
Pure-tone hearing screen (e.g., school or workplace)
Takes a few minutes in a quiet room with a portable audiometer. Pass/fail at a few key frequencies. Can be administered by a trained tech.
The gold standard
Comprehensive audiologic evaluation
Full audiometric battery in a sound-treated booth, performed by an audiologist. Air and bone conduction across all frequencies, speech testing, otoacoustic emissions, tympanometry. The reference for hearing diagnosis.
Why the quick test exists: screening lets you identify the patients who need the full booth eval, in settings (schools, work physicals, home visits) where a sound-treated booth isn't an option. Most patients pass the screen and never need the full workup.
