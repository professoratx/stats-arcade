Orientation

Overall accuracy is usually the first number authors report and the most commonly cited in clinical writing. It's intuitive: out of all the tests we did, what fraction came back correct?

But it's also the most likely number to mislead you — especially when prevalence is unusual or when the two error types matter differently. A high accuracy can hide a real problem.

Let's walk through what overall accuracy actually counts, and then explore why two very different tests can produce the same headline number.

Accuracy = 0.92That's the proportion: 462 / 500 = 0.924, rounded to 0.92 (or 92%). It's the simplest summary of how often the test got it right — and that simplicity is exactly the trap.
CI [0.90, 0.94]The interval is narrow because n is large (500). It tells us the true accuracy in this population is plausibly between 90% and 94%. Note: a tight CI means the estimate is precise — it doesn't mean the test is good.
2
What accuracy actually counts — and what it ignores

Accuracy is the fraction of patients the test classified correctly. It treats every error the same way: a missed case (FN) and a false alarm (FP) each count as one wrong answer, regardless of how different their clinical consequences are.

Because of that, two very different tests can produce the same overall accuracy. Here's the trap, side by side.

Both tests classify 462 of 500 patients correctly. But the kinds of mistakes they make are very different:

  • Test A misses 30 truly infected patients (FN = 30) but only false-alarms on 8 healthy ones. Strong for ruling in disease, weaker for ruling it out.
  • Test B misses only 10 truly infected patients (FN = 10) but false-alarms on 28. Stronger rule-out, but more healthy patients sent for unnecessary follow-up.

Overall accuracy alone cannot distinguish these two tests. That's why every paper reports more than just accuracy — you'll meet sensitivity and specificity in Parts 4 and 5, the two numbers that crack accuracy open and tell you which kind of mistake the test makes.

★ So what does Accuracy = 0.92 mean?

Out of every 100 symptomatic patients tested, the antigen test classifies about 92 correctly — either as truly infected or truly uninfected. That sounds great. But because accuracy treats all errors equally, you don't yet know which kind of mistake the test makes. Are the 8 errors mostly false alarms, or mostly missed cases? You can't tell from accuracy alone, and the difference matters enormously for clinical practice.

4
Four rules to remember when reading any accuracy

Overall accuracy shows up in nearly every diagnostic accuracy paper. These four rules carry through whatever test you're reading about:

• Accuracy = (TP + TN) / Total. Both correct cells, divided by everyone tested.
• Accuracy alone can hide a poorly-performing test. Always look for Sn and Sp alongside it.
• Accuracy depends on prevalence. A test's accuracy can change dramatically if you move it to a population with very different disease prevalence — even when Sn and Sp stay constant.
• The ~70% threshold isn't magic, but it's a useful floor. Below 70%, a test isn't generally useful clinically. Above 70%, you still need to ask: useful for what?

The most important habitNever quote overall accuracy without also quoting Sn and Sp. They're the three numbers that travel together.

Big takeaways

What overall accuracy will tell you in any paper

  • Accuracy = (TP + TN) / Total. The fraction of patients the test classified correctly — same formula no matter what condition or test you're reading about.
  • Accuracy weighs all errors equally. A missed case (FN) and a false alarm (FP) each count once. That's exactly why accuracy alone can be misleading — clinically, those two errors are very different.
  • Two tests can share the same accuracy and behave very differently. Always read accuracy alongside sensitivity and specificity. They're the three numbers that travel together.
  • Accuracy depends on who you tested. The same test in a population with different prevalence can produce a different accuracy — even though the test itself didn't change. You'll see why in Part 3.
  • Above 70% is a starting point, not a verdict. The 70% threshold rules out genuinely poor tests. Above it, the question becomes: good for ruling in, ruling out, or both? Sn and Sp answer that.
