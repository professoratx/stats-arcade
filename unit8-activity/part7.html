1
A new scenario — same six statistics

Cognitive screening is a routine part of primary care for older adults, and a flagged screen often triggers referral for an OT cognitive evaluation, ADL safety assessment, or driving review. Brief screens are quick, low-cost, and easy to administer — but they're not diagnostic on their own. The reference standard for early dementia is a comprehensive neuropsychological battery (NPB) performed by a licensed neuropsychologist.

Below is the kind of diagnostic accuracy article you'll see in occupational therapy, geriatrics, and audiology journals when a new screening tool is being evaluated. As you read, notice that all six statistics from Parts 2–6 are reported — and that the values themselves are quite different from the antigen test.

Mock journal article
Diagnostic accuracy of a brief cognitive screen for early dementia in community-dwelling older adults at primary care
Methods

Community-dwelling adults aged 65+ at three primary care clinics were screened with a 10-minute brief cognitive screen between January and June. All screened patients received a comprehensive neuropsychological battery (NPB) within 30 days, regardless of screen result. Examiners were blinded to screen results. The NPB result served as the gold standard.

Results — Statistics

Of 400 patients screened, 80 (20%) met diagnostic criteria for early dementia on NPB (prevalence = 0.20). The screen correctly identified 60 of 80 dementia cases (sensitivity = 0.75, 95% CI [0.65, 0.84]) and correctly cleared 288 of 320 patients without dementia (specificity = 0.90, 95% CI [0.86, 0.93]). Among screen-positive patients, 60 of 92 had dementia confirmed (PPV = 0.65, 95% CI [0.55, 0.74]). Among screen-negative patients, 288 of 308 were correctly cleared (NPV = 0.94, 95% CI [0.90, 0.96]). Overall accuracy was 0.87, 95% CI [0.83, 0.90].

Discussion

The brief cognitive screen demonstrates moderate sensitivity and good specificity in primary care. However, with only about two-thirds of positive screens confirmed by NPB, a positive screen is best understood as a flag for confirmatory neuropsychological testing rather than a diagnosis. A negative screen offers reasonable reassurance for asymptomatic patients but should not preclude further evaluation when clinical concern persists.

4
Four reading rules for any diagnostic accuracy paper

These four rules pull together everything from Parts 2–6 into a checklist you can use any time you read a diagnostic accuracy study:

• Sn and Sp tell you about the test. PPV and NPV tell you about the result. Sn/Sp describe how the test behaves on diseased vs. disease-free patients; PPV/NPV translate a specific positive or negative result into “what does this mean for this patient?”
• Always check whether your population's prevalence matches the study's. PPV and NPV shift with prevalence, so a study's reported PPV doesn't transfer cleanly to a setting with different prevalence.
• A weak PPV doesn't mean the test is bad — it tells you positives need confirmation. A screen with PPV = 0.65 isn't broken. It's a screen, doing its job: flagging patients who need confirmatory work-up.
• The 70% threshold is a useful floor for Sn and Sp. Below 70%, a negative (or positive) result isn't trustworthy enough on its own. Above 70%, useful; above 90%, very useful.

Big takeaways

What carries forward to every diagnostic accuracy paper

  • The same six statistics describe any diagnostic test. The math doesn't change with the disease — it's the same framework whether you're reading about an antigen test, a cognitive screen, a hearing screening, or a falls-risk tool.
  • Sn and Sp tell you about the test; PPV and NPV tell you about the result. Sn/Sp answer “how does this test behave on diseased vs. disease-free patients?” PPV/NPV answer “given my patient's actual result, what's the probability it's right?”
  • Prevalence shapes PPV and NPV. Always ask whether your patient population resembles the study's. A study's PPV is only as portable as its prevalence.
  • A weak PPV doesn't mean the test is bad. It tells you positives need confirmation. That's what screening tools are designed to do — flag, not diagnose.
  • Reading any diagnostic accuracy paper means asking the same questions. How sensitive? How specific? In whom? What does prevalence look like in my setting? What are the consequences of positive vs. negative results for this particular patient?
