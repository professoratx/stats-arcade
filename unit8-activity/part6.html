Orientation

PPV and NPV answer the patient's question. Sensitivity and specificity describe the test in the abstract, but a patient who's just received a result wants to know one thing: "is my result right?"

Sn and Sp tell you about the test. PPV and NPV tell you what a specific result means for a specific patient. They translate the test's column-based behavior (Sn, Sp) into the row-based, patient-relevant probabilities clinicians and patients actually need. That's the patient's question — finally answered.

The article reports PPV = 0.90 and NPV = 0.93 — both well above the 70% threshold for the population studied. The catch you'll learn here: those numbers shift with prevalence, so they don't always travel cleanly from one clinical setting to another.

PPV CI [0.81, 0.95]This is wider than Sp's CI [0.96, 0.99] because PPV's denominator (the 78 positive tests) is much smaller than Sp's denominator (400 disease-free patients). Smaller subgroup, more uncertainty about the true PPV. Plausibly anywhere from 0.81 to 0.95.

NPV CI [0.90, 0.95]Tighter than PPV's because the "tested negative" subgroup is much larger (422 patients vs. 78 positives). Larger denominator, more precise estimate — the true NPV is plausibly between 0.90 and 0.95.
2
The patient's question — finally answered

Look at the 2 × 2 table on the left panel. It hasn't changed since Part 5 — same TP, FP, FN, TN. What changes is which slice of the table you read.

  • Sn answers "of all the truly diseased patients, how many does the test catch?" — uses the disease+ column.
  • Sp answers "of all the truly disease-free patients, how many does the test correctly clear?" — uses the disease− column.
  • But a patient asks something different: "I just got my result. Is it right?"
  • That question is answered by PPV (if the result was positive) or NPV (if the result was negative).
  • PPV and NPV depend on the cells in the row of the patient's result — and on prevalence, because the row totals depend on prevalence.
Columns vs. Rows

Sn and Sp live in the columns of the 2 × 2 (truth columns). PPV and NPV live in the rows (test-result rows). On the left panel, the antigen-positive row is outlined in purple (PPV); the antigen-negative row is outlined in green (NPV).

Same 2 × 2 table. Sn and Sp use the columns. PPV and NPV use the rows. That's why they behave differently when prevalence changes — column proportions stay locked (Sn and Sp don't move), but row totals shift, which changes PPV and NPV. For example, the article's PPV = 0.90 only holds at the studied prevalence of 0.20 — you'll see it crater at low prevalence and climb at high prevalence in Q4 and Q5.

★ So what do PPV = 0.90 and NPV = 0.93 mean?

For the patient population studied: about 9 of every 10 positive antigen tests are real COVID, and about 93 of every 100 negative tests truly don't have COVID. That's a strong test for translating results into patient-relevant probabilities — both above the 70% threshold. The catch is that these numbers depend on this study's prevalence (0.20). 4
Four rules for reading PPV and NPV in any paper

PPV and NPV appear on most diagnostic accuracy papers, often right next to Sn and Sp. These four rules carry through whatever test you're reading about:

• PPV = TP / (TP + FP). NPV = TN / (FN + TN). PPV uses the test-positive row; NPV uses the test-negative row. Both are row-based.
• PPV and NPV translate test results into patient-relevant probabilities. They answer "given my result, what's the chance it's right?" — the question Sn and Sp can't answer directly.
• PPV and NPV depend on prevalence. Sn and Sp don't. That's the most important difference between the two pairs of statistics.
• A study's PPV and NPV apply directly only when your population's prevalence matches the study's. If it doesn't, recompute or generalize cautiously — the same test will give you different patient-meaning numbers.

The most important habitWhenever you see a study's PPV or NPV, ask "what was the prevalence in the study, and does my clinical setting look like that?" If yes, the numbers transfer. If no, the same test will produce a different PPV and NPV in your population — sometimes dramatically different.

Big takeaways

What PPV and NPV will tell you in any paper

  • PPV = TP / (TP + FP). Among patients who test positive, the fraction who actually have the disease. The test-positive row only.
  • NPV = TN / (FN + TN). Among patients who test negative, the fraction who actually don't have the disease. The test-negative row only.
  • Both answer the patient's actual question: "given my result, what's the chance it's right?" Sn and Sp can't answer that directly — PPV and NPV do.
  • Both depend on prevalence. Same test, different population, different PPV and NPV. Sn and Sp stay locked while PPV and NPV move.
  • Don't quote a study's PPV or NPV to your patient unless your clinical setting matches theirs in prevalence. When it doesn't, recompute from the 2 × 2 or generalize cautiously.
