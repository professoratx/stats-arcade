Optional review · Re-read the article Diagnostic accuracy of an over-the-counter rapid antigen test for SARS-CoV-2
Methods (abbreviated)

Prospective study of 500 symptomatic adults at a community urgent-care clinic. Each patient received a rapid antigen test followed by a reference RT-PCR test (the gold standard). Results were blinded to one another.

Results (abbreviated)

Of 500 patients, 100 (20%) were PCR-positive (prevalence = 0.20). The antigen test correctly classified 462 of 500 (accuracy = 0.92). It correctly identified 70 of 100 PCR-positive patients (Sn = 0.70) and correctly cleared 392 of 400 PCR-negative patients (Sp = 0.98). Among 78 with a positive antigen result, 70 were truly infected (PPV = 0.90). Among 422 with a negative antigen result, 392 were truly uninfected (NPV = 0.93).

Conclusion (abbreviated)

The antigen test showed high specificity but moderate sensitivity. A positive antigen result is highly trustworthy; a negative result does not reliably exclude SARS-CoV-2 in symptomatic patients. Follow-up RT-PCR is recommended when clinical suspicion remains high.
