Orientation

Prevalence doesn't measure the test — it measures the population the test is being used in. It's the proportion of patients who actually have the condition.

But here's the catch that surprises most clinicians: even though the test itself hasn't changed at all — same antigen kit, same Sn, same Sp — use it in a low-prevalence setting and a high-prevalence setting and a positive result means very different things in each. Prevalence bends every interpretation of the test result.

That's the lever this part is about.

Sn and Sp don't move — they're properties of the test itself, not the population. But PPV and NPV swing wildly:

  • In the asymptomatic screening setting (1% prevalence), a positive antigen result is wrong about three-quarters of the time (PPV = 0.26) — even though the test has 98% specificity.
  • In the outbreak ward (50% prevalence), a negative result is wrong almost a quarter of the time (NPV = 0.77) — even though the test has 70% sensitivity.

Same test. Same Sn and Sp. Completely different real-world meaning. That's the central lesson of Part 3: a test's accuracy values must be read in context of who's being tested.

4
Four rules for reading prevalence in any paper

Prevalence shows up early in nearly every diagnostic accuracy paper. These four rules let you spot when an author's PPV/NPV claims will translate to your clinic, and when they won't:

• Prevalence = (truly positive) / (total tested). A simple proportion describing the study population, not the test.
• Prevalence is local. A study's prevalence is shaped by who they recruited (symptomatic vs. asymptomatic, age, setting, time period). Your clinic's prevalence may be very different.
• Sn and Sp don't change with prevalence. They're test properties — what proportion of truly positive patients the test catches, and what proportion of truly negative patients it correctly clears.
• PPV and NPV always change with prevalence. Even with the same test. That's why journal-reported PPV and NPV always come with the study's prevalence attached — without that anchor, the numbers don't transfer.

The most important habitBefore you trust an article's PPV or NPV in your own setting, ask: is the prevalence in my population similar? If not, the same test will perform differently for you.

Big takeaways

What prevalence will tell you in any paper

  • Prevalence = (truly positive) / (total tested). The proportion of patients in the study sample with the condition. Always reported early in a diagnostic accuracy paper because it shapes everything else.
  • Prevalence describes the population, not the test. A test's prevalence will look different in different settings — symptomatic vs. asymptomatic, surge vs. calm, primary care vs. specialty clinic.
  • Sn and Sp don't move with prevalence. They're test properties. The same antigen kit catches 70% of true cases whether it's used in an outbreak or a workplace screening program.
  • PPV and NPV always move with prevalence. Same test, different population, different PPV and NPV. That's why these numbers always travel with the study's prevalence.
  • Match the article's setting to yours, or recalculate. Before applying a study's PPV or NPV to your patients, ask whether your population's prevalence is similar. If not, the headline numbers won't transfer.
