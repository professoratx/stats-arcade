What do these numbers actually mean for your patients?

Journal articles use numbers like relative risk, odds ratios, and NNT to describe whether a treatment works. These might look like different statistics, but they all come from the same simple four-cell table. Choose a scenario, enter your numbers, and then explore each measure one at a time to see what it tells you — and what it leaves out.

How to use this tool: Start by choosing a clinical scenario below (or select "Free Play" to enter your own numbers). Review the 2×2 table and click Calculate. Four metric cards will appear — click each one in order to answer an interpretation question and discover what each number means. The tool guides you through one metric at a time so you can see how each one tells a different part of the same story.
How to interpret: After you answer each question, you'll see two interpretation boxes: one shows the formal language you'd encounter in a journal article, and the other translates that into plain clinical language about what it means for patient care. The key insight: Relative Risk can make a treatment sound impressive, but Absolute Risk Reduction and NNT tell you how much it actually matters in practice.

A note on Odds Ratios: You'll see odds ratios most often in case-control studies and logistic regression. When the outcome is relatively rare (roughly <10% of the sample), the OR is very close to the RR and you can interpret them the same way. When the outcome is common, the OR will exaggerate the effect compared to RR. For this course, the most important thing is: OR > 1 means higher odds in the treatment group, OR < 1 means lower odds, and OR = 1 means no difference.
