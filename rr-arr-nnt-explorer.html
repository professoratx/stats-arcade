What's the difference between RR, ARR, and NNT?

Three numbers. One study. Each answers a different question about whether a treatment works, a prevention program is effective, or a risk factor matters. Drag the sliders and discover why one number is never enough.

How to use this tool: Start with the headline below. Then choose a context and clinical scenario, adjust the numbers in the table or with the sliders, and watch how every visual updates in real time. Click any definition card below to expand it and review what each measure means.
÷Relative Risk (RR)▼
The relative risk compares the rate of an outcome in one group to the rate in the other. Think of it as: how many times more (or less) likely is this outcome in Group 1?
How to read it:RR = 1 — No difference between groupsRR > 1 — Higher rate in Group 1RR < 1 — Lower rate in Group 1
↔Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR)▼
The ARR is the actual gap in rates between the two groups, measured in percentage points. A "2x increase" could mean 1% vs. 2% or 30% vs. 60% — the ARR tells you which.
How to read it:ARR = 0% — No difference between groupsLarger ARR — Bigger real-world gapSmaller ARR — Smaller real-world gap (even if RR looks big)
👤NNT / NNH▼
How many patients must receive the treatment for one additional patient to benefit (NNT)? Or how many must be exposed for one additional person to be harmed (NNH)?
How to read it:NNT = 1 — Every patient benefits (rare)Smaller NNT — Bigger per-person impactLarger NNT — Many patients needed per benefit
